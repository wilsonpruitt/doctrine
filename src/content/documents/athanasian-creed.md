The Athanasian Creed is the strangest of the three so-called
"ecumenical" creeds. It is not by Athanasius — it was written in
Latin, in the West, roughly a century and a half after his death,
most likely in southern Gaul around the year 500. It was never used
in the East, which to this day does not receive it (and could not,
since verse 23 confesses the *filioque*). It is not, strictly, a
creed at all in the form of the Apostles' or Nicene: it is not
interrogatory, not conciliar, not baptismal. It is a hymn-like
catechetical instruction — forty-four terse, balanced verses that
state the doctrine of the Trinity and the Incarnation with a
precision the other two creeds never attempt, and that frame the
whole with three solemn warnings that whoever does not hold this
faith "without doubt shall perish everlastingly."

Those warning clauses — the *comminatory* or *damnatory* clauses —
are why this creed is the most contested document in the catholic
tradition. They are the reason the Eastern church kept its distance,
the reason the Victorian Church of England nearly tore itself apart
over it in the 1870s, and the reason John Wesley struck the creed
entirely from the *Sunday Service of the Methodists in North America*
in 1784, so that it has never had any place in American Methodist
worship or doctrine. To annotate the Athanasian Creed for a Wesleyan
readership is therefore to annotate a document our own tradition
deliberately set down — and to ask, clause by clause, what was being
set down with it, and what was lost.

Each phrase below is annotated in the original Latin (the creed has
no Greek original) with the canonical English of the 1662 *Book of
Common Prayer* and the 1979 contemporary text alongside. The creed
divides cleanly into two parts — the Trinity (verses 3–28) and the
Incarnation (verses 29–43) — bound front, middle, and end by the
warning clauses that are its genius and its scandal.
