The Articles of Religion are the strangest confession in this
collection, because they are not, properly, a confession at all.
They are an *abridgment* of one. In 1784, preparing the *Sunday
Service of the Methodists in North America* for the new church Coke
was carrying across the Atlantic, John Wesley took the Church of
England's Thirty-Nine Articles of 1571 — the doctrinal settlement of
Tudor Christianity, Cranmer's and Parker's and Elizabeth's — and cut
them down to twenty-four. The Christmas Conference at Baltimore
adopted them and added one more, on the rulers of the United States,
for a total of twenty-five. No council wrote them. No Methodist body
debated them clause by clause. They are a sixteenth-century Anglican
text, edited by one eighteenth-century priest with a pen and a sea
voyage in view, and they have been the constitutional doctrinal
standard of American Methodism ever since.

That genre — received and edited, not composed — is the key to
reading them, and it is the same key the creeds in this collection
required. Wesley did to the Thirty-Nine Articles exactly what he did
to the Athanasian Creed: he received a catholic inheritance and
exercised editorial judgment on it, keeping, compressing, and
striking. With the creeds the editing was mostly subtraction at the
edges. Here it was structural. He removed roughly fifteen of the
Thirty-Nine. And the deletions are not incidental tidying; they are,
as in the Athanasian case, *the Wesleyan confession stated by
absence*. What is gone tells you what kind of church this was meant
to be at least as loudly as what remains.

The gravest absence is Article XVII. The Thirty-Nine Articles
contain a long, carefully balanced article *Of Predestination and
Election*. Wesley cut it entirely. The single most consequential act
of his abridgment is a deletion: American Methodism's foundational
doctrinal standard is the Tudor settlement *with its Calvinism
surgically removed*. An Arminian church was constituted not by a new
article affirming free grace for all but by the silent excision of
the article that taught election — the same theological instinct
that, in the General Rules, set a *desire* and not a doctrine at the
door, and that, in the Athanasian annotations, would not damn by
formula. Other cuts run the same direction: the descent-into-hell
clause trimmed; the article requiring the three creeds (the
Athanasian among them) gone, consistent with his striking that creed
from the very same *Sunday Service*; the articles on the authority of
general councils, on the homilies, on the consecration of bishops,
on the traditions and discipline of the church — the apparatus of
establishment — removed for a church that would have no establishment
to defend. And one article was not cut but *replaced*: where the
English Articles confessed the royal supremacy, the Christmas
Conference wrote, in its place, an article on the rulers of the
United States. The clearest possible sign that this is a living,
politically situated, edited text — the same kind of seam the
General Rules showed when "London" became "Europe."

What remained is genuinely catholic and genuinely Protestant: the
Trinity and the two natures (I–IV), the sufficiency of Scripture for
salvation (V–VI), original sin and the bondage of the will and
justification by faith alone (VII–IX), good works as the fruit of
faith and not its ground (X–XII), a Reformed-shaped doctrine of the
church and the sacraments against both Rome and the radical
Reformation (XIII–XXII), and the Christian's life under civil order,
property, and oath (XXIII–XXV). It is a lean, irenic, deliberately
*minimal* doctrinal core — Wesley kept what he thought necessary to
salvation and the catholic faith and let the contested rest fall
away. The Articles are not a comprehensive systematic theology; they
are a floor, and the floor is the point.

There is one more absence, and the church itself names it. The
Discipline's own historical statement concedes that the Articles
"lacked several Wesleyan emphases, such as assurance and Christian
perfection" — which is why Wesley's *Sermons* and *Notes*, not the
Articles, carried the distinctively Methodist teaching. The
constitutional Articles I–XXV contain no article on
*sanctification*. The single most Wesleyan doctrine in Christendom is
missing from the Wesleyan church's constitutional confession. In
1939, at the Uniting Conference, the church felt the lack and
appended two statements after Article XXV — "Of Sanctification" and
"Of the Duty of Christians to the Civil Authority" — under an
explicit note that they are legislative enactments, *not*
constitutionally protected and *not* among the Articles the uniting
churches voted. They are annotated here, flagged for exactly that
status, because the gap and the late, lesser-status patch are
themselves a confession: the church that most prizes holiness of
heart and life did not write it into the law it could not change,
and when it tried, it could only bolt it on.

Two facts govern how the document must be read now. First, like the
General Rules, the Articles are constitutionally entrenched: the
Second Restrictive Rule of 1808 forbids the General Conference to
"establish any new standards or rules of doctrine contrary to our
present existing and established standards." Together the two
Restrictive Rules make the Articles and the General Rules the
unalterable doctrinal-and-disciplinary core of the church — a creed
no one may revise and a rule no one may revoke. Second, and in
consequence, this document is, for most Methodists, almost entirely
unread — a constitutional standard honored by entrenchment and
neglected in practice, exactly the condition the General Rules are
in. To annotate the Articles is therefore not to expound a living
debate but to recover a buried foundation, and to ask, article by
article, what a tradition bound itself to when it received another
church's confession, cut its Calvinism out, and called the remainder
its own.

Each article below is annotated in Wesley's 1784 wording alongside
the received text of the United Methodist *Book of Discipline*
(¶104), with its sixteenth-century parent in the Thirty-Nine
Articles set beside it wherever Wesley kept, compressed, rewrote, or
cut — because with this document, more than any other here, the
edit *is* the doctrine. The Wesleyan voice throughout is drawn from
Wesley's own sermons and treatises, which are the authoritative
commentary on what he meant by keeping each article he kept. The
twenty-five divide cleanly: God and the Holy Trinity (I–IV), Holy
Scripture (V–VI), sin and justification (VII–IX), the Christian life
(X–XII), the church and the sacraments (XIII–XXII), and the
Christian under civil order (XXIII–XXV).
