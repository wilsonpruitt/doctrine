The General Rules are the strangest of the Methodist doctrinal
standards, because they are a doctrinal standard with almost no
doctrine in them. The Articles of Religion confess the Trinity and
the two natures; the Confession of Faith states what the church
believes. The General Rules state, in three plain imperatives, how
a Methodist is to live: *do no harm; do good; attend upon all the
ordinances of God.* The one and only condition required for
admission to the societies is not assent to any proposition at all.
It is "a desire to flee from the wrath to come, and to be saved from
their sins." A person who could not yet say the Creed could be a
Methodist. A person who would not do no harm, do good, and use the
means of grace could not — however orthodox. That inversion is the
whole document, and it is the most characteristically Wesleyan thing
in the corpus this site annotates.

They did not begin as a constitution. Wesley is emphatic, in *A
Plain Account of the People Called Methodists*, that the people
called Methodists "had no previous design or plan at all; but every
thing arose just as the occasion offered. They saw or felt some
impending or pressing evil, or some good end necessary to be
pursued." The societies, the one condition, the classes, the
penny-a-week collection, the expulsion of disorderly walkers — each
was an improvisation under pressure that turned out, "in looking
back," to have "something in Christian antiquity likewise, very
nearly parallel thereto." The Rules themselves were written for a
specific crisis: a flagging society at Newcastle in the early months
of 1743. Wesley examined the membership, an undertaking he had to
repeat almost as soon as it was finished; 140 people left, 76 of
their own accord and 64 expelled by Wesley himself. On 6 March 1743
he recorded in his Journal, "I read over in the society the rules
which all our members are to observe, and desired everyone seriously
to consider whether he was willing to conform thereto or no." Those
were these rules. Within Wesley's lifetime the tract ran through
more than thirty editions across the British Isles, with a handful
more in New York and Philadelphia.

Their substance is not organizational but theological, and the
theology is the means of grace. *Do no harm* and *do good* are the
works of mercy; *attend upon all the ordinances of God* — public
worship, the ministry of the Word, the Lord's Supper, family and
private prayer, searching the Scriptures, fasting — are the works of
piety. Together they are the channels through which Wesley believed
sanctifying grace ordinarily reaches a person who is seriously
seeking it. This is why the document can require a desire rather
than a doctrine: the Rules are the method by which the desire is
brought into contact with the grace that answers it. Read this way —
and recent scholarship on the Rules reads it precisely this way —
the General Rules are a practical theology of sanctification, the
*Plain Account*'s "method" made into a rule of life. The genre is
not law for its own sake. It is a trellis for grace.

But a rule of life is only as honest as the life it actually
disciplined, and here the document carries the gravest clause in the
Wesleyan corpus. Wesley's English original of 1743 contains no
mention of slavery. The American Methodists added one: among the
harms to be avoided, "the buying or selling the bodies and souls of
men, women, and children, with an intention to enslave them" — the
form the modern *Book of Discipline* shortens to "Slaveholding;
buying or selling slaves." With the First Restrictive Rule of 1808,
the General Rules were made constitutionally unalterable: the
General Conference may not "revoke, alter, or change" them. A church
had thus written abolition into the one part of its constitution it
had forbidden itself to amend — and then, through compromise,
suspension, and finally the schism of 1844, did not keep it. The
clause is the document's ceiling case, and it is annotated here as
exactly that: not smoothed, not excused, read as the place where a
rule of life and the church that bound itself to it came apart.

For a Wesleyan readership this document is unlike the creeds in one
decisive respect. The creeds are an inheritance the tradition
received and, in Wesley's hands, edited — struck, abridged, carried
forward. The General Rules are not received; they are native. They
are the tradition's own first description of itself, still binding,
still constitutionally entrenched, and — for most Methodists — very
nearly unread. To annotate them is not to interpret someone else's
confession. It is to ask what this tradition bound itself to when it
described, in three sentences, what it meant to be saved.

Each unit below is annotated in Wesley's 1743 English wording
alongside the received text of the United Methodist *Book of
Discipline* (¶104), with the divergences — above all the slavery
clause — flagged where they fall. The document divides cleanly: a
narrative preamble (the rise of the society, its nature, the
classes), the one condition and the fruits that evidence it, then
the three rules with their particulars, closed by the disciplinary
clause that gives the whole its teeth — "we have delivered our own
souls."
